The Grammar of Authority: Law, Morality, and the Architecture of Power

Status: Submitted
Atharva Ballal | 2026

Abstract

This essay argues that morality is not an external standard against which law is measured, but the internal language through which institutional power stabilises itself. Challenging both natural law theory and legal positivism, it asks what makes moral and legal frameworks authoritative in the first place.

The argument begins from the observation that violence, rather than peace, has historically been the default condition of organised human life. Reductions in particular forms of violence are explained not primarily as the triumph of moral insight, but as the product of changing institutional incentives and requirements. Within these structures, moral systems function as mechanisms of stabilisation, transforming coercive compliance into internalised conviction.

Three objections are addressed. First, the essay argues that self-sacrifice remains compatible with an explanation grounded in institutional formation and psychological internalisation once incentive is understood to include psychological and existential commitments. Second, responding to Habermas, it locates the demand for justification in the cognitive architecture of a coherence-seeking species rather than in self-grounding reason. Third, it contends that the same process of moral internalisation that produces moral heroism can also produce participation in atrocity.

The essay concludes that moral condemnation becomes legally enforceable only after defeat. Moral frameworks emerge within particular institutional arrangements. The central question is therefore what conditions shape those frameworks, whose interests they serve, and what would be required to move them toward more universal application.
