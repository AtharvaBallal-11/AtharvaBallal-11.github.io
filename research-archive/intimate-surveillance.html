The Emergence of Intimate Surveillance: Mandatory Live-In Registration and the Constitutional Limits of State Knowledge

Status: Submitted
Atharva Ballal | 2026

Abstract

For over two decades, Indian law approached live-in relationships through a remedial paradigm, extending civil protections, maintenance, domestic violence remedies, and recognition of children to cohabiting partners at the point of need, without requiring advance state knowledge of the relationship's existence. Beginning with Uttarakhand's Uniform Civil Code in 2024, and replicated in Gujarat and Assam in 2026, emerging state legislation replaces this paradigm with a surveillance model built from compelled intimate disclosure, police notification, third-party reporting, reproductive monitoring, and criminal enforcement, all directed at acquiring and maintaining state knowledge about intimate relationships before any harm has been alleged.

The paper advances three claims. First, this expansion of state monitoring has not been accompanied by a corresponding expansion of substantive rights or codification of existing judicial remedies. The state acquires information, but the citizens acquire little that was not already available. Second, the regime's constitutional implications cannot be understood by examining its provisions in isolation. Third, several of the mechanisms operate in tension with the practical enjoyment of the constitutional protections the judiciary has developed over two decades, creating administrative channels that may facilitate family coercion, community moral policing, and the chilling of remedial access, effects that courts have long sought to prevent. The paper concludes that the emerging surveillance model is not a refinement of the judicial approach but is fundamentally in tension with it.
