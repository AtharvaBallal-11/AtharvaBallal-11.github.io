Regulating the Gateways: Sovereign Enforcement, Asymmetric Adoption, and the Structural Limits of India's Digital Asset Regime

Status: Submitted
Atharva Ballal | 2026

Abstract

This paper is on the theory of gateway-centric sovereign regulation as the only technically coherent framework for governing decentralised digital asset markets. Distributed ledger systems are structurally resistant to direct sovereign enforcement as they are deterritorialised, intermediary-absent, and architecturally immutable. Effective regulation therefore cannot operate at the protocol layer. It must operate at the institutional gateways where digital assets interact with the conventional financial system of exchanges, custodians, and fiat conversion points, because those gateways are the only locations where a sovereign can identify, compel, and hold to account a legal person.

India's current regulatory architecture demonstrates what happens when this principle is applied selectively. The state has imported the enforcement architecture of global standard-setting bodies such as FATF's AML obligations, Basel's capital constraints, PMLA's reporting regime, while declining to adopt the consumer protection architecture those same bodies require at the gateway level. The result is a regime of asymmetric adoption with surveillance without safeguards, taxation without legal protection, and monitoring without recourse. The $230 million WazirX breach of July 2024 was not an unforeseen failure. It was the foreseeable structural consequence of that asymmetry.

Drawing on gateway-level regulatory models in Singapore, the UAE, and the EU, this paper proposes a framework for India organised around licensing, statutory custody, functional unbundling, and fiscal rationalisation. The argument throughout is that decentralised finance does not foreclose state sovereignty, rather it restructures the locations at which sovereignty can effectively operate.
