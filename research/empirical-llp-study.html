Partner Liability in Distressed Limited Liability Partnerships: An Empirical Assessment

Status: Working Draft
Atharva Ballal | Last Updated: June 27, 2026

Abstract

This study conducts an empirical investigation into partner liability exposure inside insolvent or distressed Limited Liability Partnerships (LLPs) in the United States over the past fifteen years. While statutory default law guarantees full shielding from partnership-level obligations, courts have increasingly applied veil-piercing and direct-liability exceptions to claw back distributions from equity partners. By analyzing case filings across major jurisdictions, we identify the primary legal and economic triggers that lead to shield-penetration in practice.

Research Question

Under what empirical conditions do courts bypass statutory LLP liability shields to hold individual law, accounting, or professional partners personally liable for firm-level obligations during restructuring or liquidation?

Methods

We construct a dataset of 342 state and federal court opinions related to LLP insolvency between 2010 and 2025. Opinions are coded qualitatively for arguments concerning fraudulent transfer, breach of fiduciary duty, direct tort liability, and traditional veil-piercing. We utilize quantitative regression models to correlate shield failure with specific variables, such as capital contribution adequacy and firm-restructuring timelines.

Current Progress

The data cleaning stage is complete. We are currently drafting the findings section, focusing on the statistically significant variance in courts' handling of the "contribution exception" in Delaware vs. New York law.
